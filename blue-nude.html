Matisse began cutouts in the 1940s. The four Blue Nudes above were all based on the same pose, a nude female with intertwined legs and her arm over her head, thus why they have the same name but with increasing numbers.

The Life of Matisse

Matisse was born in Northern France in 1869. He originally went to study law in 1887, but then found his passion in painting, and went to École Nationale des Beaux-Arts in Paris to study art.

While Matisse was first taught traditional Classicism with still lifes and realistic portrayals, he was later introduced to Impressionism, an art style that had visible brush strokes and an unusual blending of colors to create a sort of vibration. Matisse experimented with Impressionism, definitely inspiring his later use of bright colors.

Still Life with Compote, Apples and Oranges (1899)

Matisse traveled to multiple countries in Africa and Oceania, where the people, local art, and landscapes inspired him further in abstraction. He would buy and collect 'antiques' from these countries, such as African sculptures and Polynesian rugs.

Arab Coffeehouse (1912-1913)

From his trip to the Tahitian islands in 1930 and gaining stomach cancer in 1941, Matisse started doing only cutout pieces. Matisse had to use a wheelchair and was often bedbound, so cutting paper (with the help of his studio assistants) was his best option to continue his art, until his passing in 1954.

Inspiration and Artworks

Matisse before was an impressionist artist, such as with his first Blue Nude (1907)

Matisse was also a founder in Fauvism, which prioritized color, especially using color straight out of the tube. We can see it in his background for Bathers with a Turtle (1907)

His journey in rejecting the canon European art and more into his "Modern" art, Matisse took inspiration from his African sculpture. (below are his purchased Jomooniw Male and Female Figures) by a Bamana artist.

From his trip to the Tahitian islands in 1930 and his revelation in Islamic art, Matisse was inspired into crating his cut out piece—specifically, the shapes and lineless forms replicated from Polynesian and Islam art. A great example is his tapestry piece Oceania, The Sky (1946) which tries to replicate the cloths he collected from Oceania islands.

The Process of the Cutouts

First, Matisse painted a big paper canvas with one color, straight out of the tube.

He then (with the help of his assistants) cut out fluid and organic shapes with scissors

With pins, he placed the cut out shapes onto a bigger paper canvas, repeating the process until he was satisfied. After he found the final position, he glued the cutout goache-painted paper onto the background/canvas paper.
